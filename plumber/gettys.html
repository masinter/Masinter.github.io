(Originally published in InfoWorld Electric.)

Now that http:// is not only ubiquitous but hip, the World Wide Web's HTTP accounts for 70 percent of Internet traffic. Sure, be grateful for this Internet plumbing that downloads your many wonderful Web pages, but be aware that in doing so, HTTP generates far too many unnecessary Internet packets. HTTP is one of the main reasons you often have to use your Web browser's prominent Stop button. HTTP 1.0 is seriously bogging the Internet down. UUNet's Mike O'Dell, last year's winner, refers to the currently deployed 1.0 version of HTTP as "the Internet's bug number one." O'Dell has high praise for this year's Internet Plumber of the Year, Jim Gettys, for his work on the coming 1.1 version of HTTP, which is now in its final rounds of standardization, implementation, and deployment.

Sharing the credit

Jim Gettys wouldn't at first let InfoWorld give him our 1997 Internet Plumber of the Year. I had to agree to credit the whole team working on HTTP/1.1. Then Gettys had a hard time limiting the list of HTTP/1.1 team members and is clearly bent on sharing credit, which has to be one of the secrets of his continuing success.

According to Gettys, big contributors to HTTP/1.1 include Roy Fielding, at the University of California at Irvine, for putting the initial proposal together; Jeff Mogul, at Digital Equipment's Western Research Lab, for arguing persistent connections and caching; Henrik Frystyk Nielsen, at the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), for serious design work and early implementations; Paul Leach, at Microsoft, for widespread contributions; Dave Kristol, at Lucent, for dealing with the cookie mess; and John Franks for digest authentication.

Larry Massinter at the Xerox Palo Alto Research Center chairs the HTTP/1.1 working group of the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF at http://www.ietf.org). When I asked Massinter what he thought about InfoWorld recognizing Gettys, he quipped, "I'm happy to be the plumber's helper."

Gettys says there are many more HTTP/1.1 contributors, but I had to cut the list off at that point. If you've been left out, sorry -- it's my fault, and you're in good company with W3C's Tim Berners-Lee, also an HTTP/1.1 contributor and, by the way, the inventor of the Web.

So who is Jim Gettys, and what is HTTP/1.1?

Gettys is employed by Digital, which is reported to be considering him for promotion in its Industry Standards and Consortia activity -- hope this award helps, Jim. For years, Gettys has been a visiting scientist at W3C at Massachusetts Institute of Technology's (MIT's) Laboratory for Computer Science. Of course, Gettys has his own Web pages at http://www.w3.org/People/Gettys.

Before, Gettys was best known as co-inventor (with Bob Scheifler) of MIT's X Window System.

Gettys says he got much of his network applications experience working on X, which is a "network transparent window system in which you can run applications on any machine in a network, something that Microsoft can't say." Gettys is proud of X and should be. I think of X as the previous generation, just before the Web -- of thin-client Internet software. Now Gettys is back with HTTP/1.1.

Harvard's Scott Bradner, another prominent Internet plumber, agrees that Gettys' work on HTTP/1.1 is pivotal.

"Jim Gettys has been able to help bring peace to the IETF/W3C standards picture," Bradner says. "He has just the right ability to get these types of ego-filled organizations to agree on how to divide up the work -- a very important ability if we are to make progress rather than making redundant protocol proposals." Bradner suggests we be clear that Gettys' HTTP work is done under the auspices of the IETF, not the W3C, not Digital, and certainly not MIT's Laboratory for Computer Science -- spoken like the 32-year Harvard man that Bradner is. You can find more about HTTP/1.1 at http://www.w3.org/Protocols.

In short, HTTP is the protocol by which your Web browser downloads pages when you give it a URL. Unfortunately, HTTP/1.0 opens and closes a TCP connection for each element of each Web page -- each block of text, each graphic -- and there are lots of them. HTTP downloading generates a large number of TCP opening and closing IP packets, which carry no data and waste a lot of time. Further, the connections that HTTP opens give TCP's congestion-control mechanisms too little time to work. The result is that all-too-familiar bogging down of Web response, many lost packets, and frequent recourse to your browser's Stop button. And HTTP's abuse of TCP/IP is only the beginning.

Next-generation HTTP

When the Web was only a physicist's dream in 1990, HTTP/1.0 was just fine, and that's a gross understatement. But as Marc Andreessen, Netscape, and you all have taken the Web to its dizzying heights, HTTP/1.0 sticks out like a sore thumb. Hence the need for the IETF to develop a "next generation" of HTTP (called HTTPng at http://www.ietf.org), and in the meantime, a very important upgrade called HTTP/1.1.

We gathered the HTTP/1.1 team on a large conference call and asked them what would do the most good for the Internet's leaky plumbing. They said I should encourage you to ask your suppliers of Internet server software and browsers to deploy good implementations of HTTP/1.1. Ask for it by name. Various implementations of HTTP/1.1 are now available in the major Web browsers and servers.

But according to Gettys, it is too easy to do "a compliant implementation of HTTP/1.1 which has most of the bad properties of 1.0 ... a good implementation does persistent connections, pipelining, and buffering." Not only does a good implementation of HTTP/1.1 reduce the Internet-clogging packets generated by your Web downloads -- by 50 percent to 90 percent -- but when your HTTP/1.1-equipped browser hits an HTTP/1.1-equipped server, you'll see a substantial speeding up of Web access, reducing download times by 20 percent to 75 percent, Gettys says.

So with your newfound extra time, send your congratulations to Gettys at jg@pa.dec.com.
